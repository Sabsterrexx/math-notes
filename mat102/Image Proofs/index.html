How to do an Image/Range Proof

Review

If we have a function denoted as \(f\), defined as \(f: D \to C\), then \(D\) is the domain, and \(C\) is the codomain. The image of the function is

\[ f(D) = \{f(x) \mid x \in D\} \]

(the set of all outputs of the function for every input in the domain).

Example

If we have a function:

\[ f: \{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\} \to \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}, \quad f(x) = x^2 \]

We describe the domain and the codomain as the following sets:

Domain (\(D\)):

\[ \{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\} \]

Codomain:

\[ \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\} \]

The range (or image) of the function is:

\[ f(D) = \{ f(x) \mid x \in D \} \]

Since

\[ D = \{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\}, \]

we can evaluate:

\[ f(\{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\}) = \{ f(x) \mid x \in \{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\} \} \]
\[ = \{ x^2 \mid x \in \{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2\} \} \]
\[ = \{(-2)^2, (-1)^2, 0^2, 1^2, 2^2\} \]
\[ = \{4, 1, 0\} \]

Note: "range" and "image" mean the same thing. You should also notice with this example that even though the image is a subset of the codomain, it may not always be equal to it.

Proof Strategies

Suppose we have a function

\[ f: A \to B \]

and we want to prove its range (or image) is \(C\) (with \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) being arbitrary sets). Essentially, we want to show:

\[ f(A) = C \]

To prove two sets are equal, we need double subset inclusion, i.e., prove both:

\[ f(A) \subseteq C \]

and

\[ C \subseteq f(A). \]

Step 1: Show \(f(A) \subseteq C\)

This means:

\[ \forall y, \quad y \in f(A) \implies y \in C \]

Usually, this is just a simple inequality check or "definition unwinding" if \(C\) is an interval of real numbers.

Step 2: Show \(C \subseteq f(A)\)

This means:

\[ \forall y, \quad y \in C \implies y \in f(A) \]

Or equivalently:

\[ \forall y \in C, \exists x \in A \text{ such that } f(x) = y \]

Two main approaches

Approach A (for intervals of real numbers)

  1. Pick \(y \in C\)
  2. Set \(x\) to be the “inverse” of \(f\) in terms of \(y\)
  3. Show \(x \in A\)
  4. Plug \(x\) into \(f\) and show \(f(x) = y\)

Approach B (for more abstract or multi-variable functions like \(f(x,y)\))

  1. Pick \(z \in C\)
  2. Choose \(x,y\) in terms of \(z\) (sometimes you may need to resort to “fixing” one of the variables to a constant)
  3. Show \(f(x,y) = z\) by plugging in and evaluating the function with these new values

Essentially, Step 2 is about explicitly constructing a pre-image for every element in \(C\).

Example 1

Claim

Let

\[ f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \]

be defined by

\[ f(x) = x^3. \]

Then the image of \(f\) is \(\mathbb{R}\).

Meaning,

\[ f(\mathbb{R}) = \mathbb{R}. \]

Proof

Step 1: Show \(f(\mathbb{R}) \subseteq \mathbb{R}\)

Let \(y \in f(\mathbb{R})\).

Then by definition of image, there exists \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) such that

\[ y = f(x) = x^3. \]

Since \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), we have \(x^3 \in \mathbb{R}\).

Thus,

\[ y \in \mathbb{R}. \]

So,

\[ f(\mathbb{R}) \subseteq \mathbb{R}. \]

Step 2: Show \(\mathbb{R} \subseteq f(\mathbb{R})\) (Approach A)

Let \(y \in \mathbb{R}\).

We must show that there exists \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) such that

\[ f(x) = y. \]

Since

\[ f(x) = x^3, \]

solve for \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

\[ x = \sqrt[3]{y}. \]

Since \(y \in \mathbb{R}\), we have

\[ \sqrt[3]{y} \in \mathbb{R}. \]

Thus,

\[ x \in \mathbb{R}. \]

Now compute:

\[ f(x) = f(\sqrt[3]{y}) = (\sqrt[3]{y})^3 = y. \]

Thus, for every \(y \in \mathbb{R}\), there exists \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) such that

\[ f(x) = y. \]

So,

\[ \mathbb{R} \subseteq f(\mathbb{R}). \]

Since both inclusions hold, we conclude:

\[ f(\mathbb{R}) = \mathbb{R}. \]

Example 2

Claim

Let

\[ f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R} \]

be defined by

\[ f(x,y) = x + y. \]

Then the image of \(f\) is \(\mathbb{R}\).

That is,

\[ f(\mathbb{R}^2) = \mathbb{R}. \]

Proof

Step 1: Show \(f(\mathbb{R}^2) \subseteq \mathbb{R}\)

Let

\[ z \in f(\mathbb{R}^2). \]

Then there exists \((x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2\) such that

\[ z = f(x,y) = x + y. \]

Since \(x,y \in \mathbb{R}\), it follows that

\[ x+y \in \mathbb{R}. \]

Thus,

\[ z \in \mathbb{R}. \]

So,

\[ f(\mathbb{R}^2) \subseteq \mathbb{R}. \]

Step 2: Show \(\mathbb{R} \subseteq f(\mathbb{R}^2)\) (Approach B)

Let

\[ z \in \mathbb{R}. \]

We must show that there exists \((x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2\) such that

\[ f(x,y) = z. \]

Construct a pre-image as follows:

Let

\[ x = z \]

and let

\[ y = 0. \]

Then

\[ (x,y) = (z,0) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \]

since \(z \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(0 \in \mathbb{R}\).

Now compute:

\[ f(z,0) = z + 0 = z. \]

Thus, for every \(z \in \mathbb{R}\), we have constructed an element of \(\mathbb{R}^2\) that maps to it, meaning:

\[ \forall z \in \mathbb{R}, \exists (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \text{ such that } f(x,y) = z. \]

So,

\[ \mathbb{R} \subseteq f(\mathbb{R}^2). \]

Since both inclusions hold, we conclude:

\[ f(\mathbb{R}^2) = \mathbb{R}. \]

Final Note

There are certainly more strange image-proof questions you will have to do in this course (I am certain your professors are capable of making a few), but I've tried to cover the most general use-cases in this guide.

However, ultimately, keep in mind that mathematics is not a spectator sport. Do more proofs, ask your instructors for feedback if you have that luxury, and find other people to cross-check your work with.

Reading these notes and never doing the practice is like watching a 3Blue1Brown video and expecting to become an expert on some mathematics topic. You may come to appreciate the subject more, but you will be far from mastering it without rigorous study.
